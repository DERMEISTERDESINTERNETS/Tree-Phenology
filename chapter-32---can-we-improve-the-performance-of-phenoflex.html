29 Chapter 32 - Can we improve the performance of PhenoFlex?

29.1 Questions

  1. What was the objective of this work?
  2. What was the main conclusion?
  3. What experiments could we conduct to test the hypothesis that emerged at the end of the conclusion?

29.2 Answers

1. What was the objective of this work?

The objective of this work was to evaluate whether the performance of the PhenoFlex phenology model can be improved by modifying the calibration dataset. In particular, the study investigated the effect of including or excluding marginal winter seasons, which are seasons with unusually warm winter temperatures that may not represent typical dormancy conditions.

The goal was to determine whether these atypical seasons influence the estimation of model parameters and thereby affect the predictive accuracy of the model. The model can look something like this:

Figure 29.1: Chill and heat response plots for two versions of the PhenoFlex model: (Luedeling et al. 2021).

2. What was the main conclusion?

The main conclusion was that including marginal seasons with unusually warm winter conditions reduced the performance of the PhenoFlex model. When these seasons were excluded, the model predicted bloom dates more accurately. This suggests that the model does not fully represent biological processes occurring under extreme temperature conditions, especially during dormancy release.

3. What experiments could be conducted to test the emerging hypothesis?

To test the hypothesis, controlled experiments should expose trees to a wide range of winter temperature conditions. For example, tree-moving or climate-chamber experiments could simulate warmer and colder winters and measure dormancy release and bloom timing. Such systematic experiments would help determine whether additional biological mechanisms influence dormancy breaking that are not currently included in the model.

References

Luedeling, Eike, Katja Schiffers, Till Fohrmann, and Carsten Urbach. 2021. “PhenoFlex - an Integrated Model to Predict Spring Phenology in Temperate Fruit Trees.” Agricultural and Forest Meteorology 307: 108491. https://doi.org/https://doi.org/10.1016/j.agrformet.2021.108491.
